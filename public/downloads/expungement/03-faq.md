# Illinois Expungement & Sealing — FAQ

Straight answers to the questions everyone searches for.

---

**Q: What's the difference between expungement and sealing?**

**Expungement:** The record is physically destroyed. It's gone — law enforcement agencies must delete it.

**Sealing:** The record still exists but is hidden from the general public, employers, and landlords. Law enforcement can still access sealed records.

Expungement is better when available. Most convictions can only be sealed, not expunged.

---

**Q: Can I legally say "no" on a job application after expungement?**

Yes. Under Illinois law (20 ILCS 2630/12), once a record is expunged, you may treat the arrest or charge as if it never occurred. You can legally answer "no" to questions about prior arrests or convictions — **except** when the application explicitly asks about sealed or expunged records (rare, but some government and law enforcement positions do this).

---

**Q: How long does the process take?**

From filing to final order: typically **4–6 months** for uncontested cases. Breakdown:
- Hearing date: 30–90 days after filing
- State's Attorney review: 30–60 days
- ISP record update after order: 60–90 days

---

**Q: Does it cost anything?**

Filing fees vary by county — typically **$0–$120**. Some counties waive fees for financial hardship.

If you hire an attorney: $500–$2,000+ depending on complexity.

This kit helps you do it yourself for just the filing fee.

---

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**

Not for straightforward cases. The Illinois court system provides forms and most clerks are helpful. This guide + checklist covers the entire process.

You may want an attorney if:
- You have multiple offenses across multiple counties
- The State's Attorney objects to your petition
- You're unsure about eligibility

Free legal help: illinoislegalaid.org, Cabrini Green Legal Aid (Chicago), Prairie State Legal Services.

---

**Q: My case was dismissed — can I expunge immediately?**

Generally, yes — arrests without conviction are immediately eligible for expungement once the case is closed. However, some cases require a waiting period (e.g., supervision sentences require 5 years after the supervision period ends).

---

**Q: What about my DUI?**

DUI convictions **cannot** be expunged or sealed in Illinois — this is explicitly excluded under 20 ILCS 2630/5.2. There are no exceptions.

If your DUI was reduced to reckless driving, reckless driving convictions are also ineligible.

---

**Q: I have convictions in multiple counties. Do I file in each one?**

Yes. You must file a separate petition in each county where you have a conviction. However, arrests without conviction filed with the Illinois State Police can sometimes be addressed in one petition regardless of county.

---

**Q: Will sealed records show on a background check?**

Most commercial background check companies and employers will NOT see sealed records. However:

- Law enforcement: yes
- State licensing boards for certain professions (nursing, teaching): varies
- Federal background checks: may appear depending on circumstances
- FBI: maintains its own records and sealed Illinois records may still appear federally

---

**Q: What about federal convictions?**

This guide only covers **Illinois state records**. Federal convictions are handled through the federal court system. There is currently no general federal expungement statute for adults — you would need a presidential pardon for federal relief.

---

**Q: Can I expunge juvenile records?**

Most juvenile records can be expunged once you turn 18 or 21 (depending on offense type). Illinois law strongly favors expunging juvenile records. Many are expunged automatically at age 21. Contact your juvenile court clerk for specific records.

---

**Q: My record shows arrests that didn't lead to charges. Can those be expunged?**

Yes. Arrests without any charge filed are typically eligible for immediate expungement. This includes arrests where the State declined to prosecute.

---

**Q: What if the State's Attorney objects?**

The State's Attorney has 60 days to object after being served. Objections are uncommon for clear-cut eligible cases. If they do object, you'll have a contested hearing where both sides present arguments to the judge. Consider consulting an attorney at that point.

---

**Q: How do I check my record after expungement?**

Run a background check on yourself through:
- Illinois State Police (official): isp.illinois.gov
- A commercial background check service (NCIC, BeenVerified, etc.)
- Wait 60–90 days after receiving the court order before checking
