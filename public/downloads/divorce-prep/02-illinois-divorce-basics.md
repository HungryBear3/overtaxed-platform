# Illinois Divorce — The Basics You Need to Know

**Illinois is a no-fault divorce state.** You don't need to prove wrongdoing — irreconcilable differences is sufficient grounds. Here's how the process works.

---

## TYPES OF DIVORCE IN ILLINOIS

### Uncontested Divorce (Joint Simplified Dissolution)
Both spouses agree on everything: property division, debt, custody, support.

**Eligible if:**
- Married less than 8 years
- No children together (or children are adults)
- No real estate owned jointly
- Combined gross income under $60,000/year
- Total assets under $50,000 (excluding cars)
- Neither spouse seeks spousal support

**Process:** File joint petition → one court appearance → done. Usually 4–8 weeks.

### Uncontested Divorce (Full, but Agreement Reached)
You've been married longer or have more assets, but you've reached a full agreement on all issues.

**Process:** File petition → prepare Marital Settlement Agreement → present to judge for approval. 3–6 months typically.

### Contested Divorce
Spouses cannot agree on one or more issues (property, custody, support). Requires discovery, potentially mediation, and possibly trial.

**Process:** Can take 1–3+ years depending on complexity and cooperation.

---

## RESIDENCY REQUIREMENT

At least one spouse must have been an **Illinois resident for at least 90 days** before filing.

---

## WHERE TO FILE

File in the **Circuit Court** of the county where either spouse lives.

---

## THE DIVORCE PROCESS (Overview)

### Step 1 — File the Petition
One spouse files a **Petition for Dissolution of Marriage** with the Circuit Court Clerk.
Filing fee: approximately **$150–$400** depending on county.

### Step 2 — Serve Your Spouse
The other spouse must be officially served with the petition (or sign an **Entry of Appearance** waiving formal service, which is common in uncontested divorces).

### Step 3 — Financial Disclosure
Both parties complete a **Financial Affidavit** disclosing all income, assets, and debts. This is mandatory in Illinois.

### Step 4 — Negotiation / Agreement
For uncontested divorces: draft and sign the **Marital Settlement Agreement** (property, debt, spousal support) and if applicable the **Parenting Plan / Parenting Agreement** (custody, visitation, child support).

For contested divorces: discovery, depositions, mediation, and potentially trial.

### Step 5 — Prove Up Hearing
A brief court appearance (5–15 minutes for uncontested cases) where the judge reviews your agreement and enters the **Judgment of Dissolution of Marriage**.

## KEY ILLINOIS DIVORCE CONCEPTS

### Division of Property — Equitable Distribution
Illinois divides **marital property** equitably (fairly, not necessarily 50/50). The court considers:
- Length of marriage
- Each spouse's financial contribution
- Each spouse's future earning capacity
- Non-marital property (kept by the original owner)
- Any dissipation of marital assets

**Marital property:** Everything acquired during the marriage (with some exceptions)
**Non-marital property:** Property owned before marriage, gifts, inheritances — generally stays with the original owner

### Spousal Maintenance (Alimony)
Not automatic. Courts consider:
- Length of marriage
- Each spouse's income and earning capacity
- Standard of living during the marriage
- Age and health of each spouse
- Childcare responsibilities

Illinois has a **maintenance formula** for marriages under 20 years:
- Amount: 33.3% of payor's net income minus 25% of recipient's net income
- Duration: based on length of marriage (e.g., 20% of marriage length for a 5-year marriage)

### Child Custody (Allocation of Parental Responsibilities)
Illinois no longer uses "custody" — it allocates:
- **Decision-making responsibilities:** Who decides on education, healthcare, religion, extracurriculars
- **Parenting time:** When each parent has the child

Standard is **best interests of the child**. Factors include child's relationship with each parent, adjustment to home/school/community, mental health of all parties, and child's wishes (considered based on age/maturity).

### Child Support
Illinois uses an **income shares model:**
1. Combine both parents' net incomes
2. Apply the statutory table (based on combined income + number of children) to get the "basic support obligation"
3. Each parent's share is proportional to their contribution to combined income

Basic support is then adjusted for healthcare, childcare, and parenting time.

## COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

1. **Hiding assets** — Illinois courts take financial disclosure seriously; concealment can result in sanctions
2. **Signing agreements without reading them** — a Marital Settlement Agreement is binding
3. **Moving out of the marital home without a plan** — may affect your rights to the home
4. **Using children as messengers** — creates evidence of bad parenting in custody disputes
5. **Social media posts** — anything public can be used as evidence
6. **Not updating beneficiary designations** — insurance, retirement accounts, wills
