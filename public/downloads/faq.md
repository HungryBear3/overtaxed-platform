# Frequently Asked Questions — Illinois Property Tax Appeals

---

**Q: Do I need an attorney to appeal my property taxes?**

No. The vast majority of residential property tax appeals are filed by homeowners without any legal help. The process is designed to be accessible. You'll need to fill out a form and submit evidence — that's it. Attorneys and tax consultants are useful for complex commercial properties or PTAB appeals, but for a standard residential appeal, this packet has everything you need.

---

**Q: How much can I actually save?**

It varies widely. Small wins are $500–$1,500/year. Larger reductions on overvalued properties can save $3,000–$10,000/year or more. Even a modest 10% reduction on a $300,000 assessed value (Cook County) would save you around $800–$1,200 per year depending on your tax rate — every year, indefinitely, until the next reassessment.

---

**Q: Will appealing cause my taxes to go up?**

No. Filing an appeal cannot result in an increase to your assessed value. The Board of Review can only reduce or maintain your assessment — they cannot raise it in response to an appeal. You have nothing to lose by filing.

---

**Q: What if my appeal is denied?**

You have options. If the county Board of Review denies your appeal, you can escalate to the Illinois Property Tax Appeal Board (PTAB) — the state-level body. You have 30 days from the denial notice to file with PTAB. PTAB appeals take longer (1–3 years) but are worth it for significant overvaluations. See the Filing Instructions document for details.

---

**Q: How long does the process take?**

County Board of Review decisions typically take 2–6 months after filing. If you escalate to PTAB, expect 1–3 years. Your tax bill will usually reflect a reduction in the following year's bill, not the current one — though some counties apply reductions retroactively.

---

**Q: My house is in Cook County — is the process different?**

Yes, slightly. Cook County uses a 10% assessment level (vs. 33.33% for all other Illinois counties), and each of Cook County's 38 townships has its own appeal window that rolls throughout the year. You must appeal during your township's open window. Check cookcountyboardofreview.com to see if your township is currently open. The good news: Cook County has a robust online filing system that makes appeals fairly straightforward.

---

**Q: What's the difference between the Assessor and the Board of Review?**

The **Assessor** sets your initial assessed value. The **Board of Review** is where you appeal that value. They are separate offices. You file your appeal with the Board of Review, not the Assessor's office. In Cook County, you can also appeal directly to the Assessor first (CCAO appeal) before going to the Board of Review — this is a separate process.

---

**Q: Do I need to attend a hearing?**

Usually not. Most residential appeals are decided on the paperwork you submit — no hearing required. However, some counties offer optional hearings, and for large reductions or complex cases, requesting a hearing can help. If a hearing is scheduled, you'll receive notice by mail.

---

**Q: Can I appeal every year?**

Yes, you can file an appeal every year during the open appeal window. However, your county only reassesses properties every 1–3 years (Cook County reassesses each township on a 3-year rotation). If there's no reassessment, your assessed value stays the same — but you can still appeal if you have new evidence.

---

**Q: What if I just bought the house?**

Your purchase price is excellent evidence. If you paid less than your assessed value implies, bring your closing documents (HUD-1 or Closing Disclosure) as evidence. A recent arm's-length sale is one of the strongest arguments for a reduction. File immediately after the purchase during the next open window.
