# Illinois Small Claims Court — FAQ

Honest answers to the questions people actually ask.

---

**Q: Do I need a lawyer?**

No. Small claims court is specifically designed for people without attorneys. In fact, having a lawyer can sometimes work against you — judges know you're representing yourself and are generally patient. The other side having a lawyer doesn't automatically mean you lose.

---

**Q: How much can I sue for?**

Up to **$10,000** in Illinois small claims court. If your damages exceed that, you can either limit your claim to $10,000 (and waive the rest) or file in regular civil court (which is more complex and usually benefits from an attorney).

---

**Q: How long does it take?**

- Filing to hearing: typically **30–60 days**
- The hearing itself: usually **10–30 minutes**
- Judgment: same day or within a few weeks by mail

---

**Q: What if the defendant doesn't show up?**

You win by default. The judge will likely enter a **default judgment** in your favor for the amount you claimed. Bring your evidence anyway — the judge may ask questions before granting it.

---

**Q: What if I lose?**

You have **30 days to appeal** to the Illinois Appellate Court. Appeals are more complex and usually involve filing a brief. Consider whether the amount at stake justifies the additional time and possible attorney cost.

Your filing fee is generally not refunded if you lose, but it's only $75–$200.

---

**Q: Can the defendant countersue me?**

Yes. In small claims court, a defendant can file a counterclaim at or before the hearing. If they do, both claims are heard at the same time. This is rare for straightforward disputes but worth knowing.

---

**Q: What if I win but they don't pay?**

Winning a judgment is step one. Collecting is step two. Options include:
- **Wage garnishment** — garnish up to 15% of their disposable earnings
- **Bank levy** — seize funds from their bank account
- **Property lien** — attach to their real estate (they can't sell without paying you)

File a **Citation to Discover Assets** (ask the clerk) to force them to reveal their finances under oath.

Judgments are enforceable for **7 years** and can be renewed.

---

**Q: Does it matter that I don't have a written contract?**

Not necessarily. Oral contracts are enforceable in Illinois up to **5 years** after the breach. Text messages, emails, and other communications can prove the agreement existed. The stronger your paper trail, the better — but lack of a signed contract isn't automatically fatal.

---

**Q: Can I sue a business?**

Yes. Sue the business by its legal name (the registered entity name, not just the "doing business as" name). Look up the registered name at the Illinois Secretary of State website (ilsos.gov). Make sure you sue the right legal entity.

---

**Q: What if the defendant is in another county?**

You generally must file where the defendant lives, works, or where the incident occurred. If they're in another county, you may need to file there. Contact that county's Circuit Court Clerk to confirm.

---

**Q: Can I claim my time and stress?**

No. Small claims courts award **economic damages** — actual financial losses. You generally cannot recover for your time spent preparing the case, emotional distress, or inconvenience. You can recover court costs (filing fees, service fees) if you win.

---

**Q: What's the statute of limitations?**

- Written contracts: **10 years** (735 ILCS 5/13-206)
- Oral contracts: **5 years** (735 ILCS 5/13-205)
- Property damage: **5 years**
- Personal injury: **2 years**

Don't wait. File before the clock runs out or you lose the right to sue entirely.

---

**Q: What should I say at the hearing?**

Keep it short and factual. Introduce yourself, state what the defendant owes you and why, present your evidence in order, and ask the judge to rule in your favor. Don't editorialize or repeat yourself. Let your documents do the talking.

A good opening: *"Your Honor, I'm here today because [defendant] owes me $[amount] for [reason]. I have [X] pieces of evidence supporting my claim."*
