# Illinois Small Claims Court — Step-by-Step Filing Instructions

**Small Claims court is designed for people without lawyers.** You can sue for up to $10,000. Here's exactly how to do it.

---

## STEP 1 — Send a Demand Letter First

Before filing, send the demand letter (included in this kit). Courts appreciate it, and it sometimes resolves the dispute without a hearing. Give the other party 10–14 days to respond.

If they ignore it or refuse — proceed to Step 2.

---

## STEP 2 — Determine Which Court to File In

File in the **Circuit Court** of the county where:
- The defendant **lives or works**, OR
- The **incident or contract** occurred

---

## STEP 3 — Complete Your Complaint

Use the Complaint Template included in this kit. Fill in:
- Your name and address (Plaintiff)
- Defendant's full name and address
- What happened and when
- The exact amount you're claiming

**Make 3 copies:**
1. Original → file with court
2. One copy → for serving the defendant
3. One copy → your records

---

## STEP 4 — File at the Courthouse

Bring to the clerk's office:
- Completed complaint (original + 2 copies)
- Filing fee (cash, check, or card — varies by county and claim size)

**Filing Fees (approximate):**

| Claim Amount | Typical Fee |
|-------------|------------|
| Up to $500 | $75–$100 |
| $500–$2,500 | $100–$150 |
| $2,500–$10,000 | $150–$200 |

The clerk will:
- Stamp your complaint
- Assign a case number
- Give you a hearing date (usually 30–60 days out)
- Give you instructions for serving the defendant

---

## STEP 5 — Serve the Defendant

The defendant must be **officially notified** of the lawsuit. Illinois offers several methods:

**Option A — Sheriff Service (most common)**
- Pay a fee (~$25–$60) for the sheriff's office to deliver the summons
- Ask the clerk how to arrange this when you file

**Option B — Certified Mail**
- Some courts allow certified mail service — ask the clerk
- Defendant must sign for it; keep the green return receipt card

**Option C — Special Process Server**
- Hire a licensed process server (search your county)
- Useful if defendant is avoiding service

**Important:** You cannot serve the defendant yourself.

---

## STEP 6 — Prepare for Your Hearing

Organize your evidence before the hearing date:

**Bring everything that supports your case:**
- [ ] Signed contracts or agreements
- [ ] Invoices and receipts
- [ ] Text messages and emails (printed or on phone)
- [ ] Photos or videos
- [ ] Bank records or payment confirmations
- [ ] Your demand letter + proof of delivery
- [ ] Any witnesses who can testify

**Organize chronologically.** Number your exhibits (Exhibit A, B, C...).

**Practice your statement.** You'll have a few minutes to explain your case to the judge. Keep it factual: what happened, when, how much you're owed, and why.

---

## STEP 7 — The Hearing

Small claims hearings are informal. The judge will:
1. Hear your side
2. Hear the defendant's side
3. Ask questions
4. Issue a judgment (sometimes same day, sometimes by mail)

**Tips:**
- Address the judge as "Your Honor"
- Stick to facts, not emotions
- Bring your organized evidence binder
- Don't interrupt the other party

---

## STEP 8 — If You Win

Winning a judgment doesn't automatically get you paid. If the defendant doesn't pay voluntarily:

**Enforcement options:**
- **Wage garnishment** — court orders their employer to withhold pay
- **Bank levy** — court orders their bank to release funds
- **Lien on property** — attach a lien to their real estate

File a "Citation to Discover Assets" to force the defendant to disclose their finances. Ask the clerk for the form.

**Judgments are good for 7 years** and can be renewed.

---

## STEP 9 — If You Lose (or Want to Appeal)

You have **30 days** to appeal a small claims judgment to the Appellate Court. Appeals are more complex and often benefit from an attorney. Consider whether the amount justifies the effort.

---

## KEY ILLINOIS LAWS TO KNOW

- **Security deposits:** 765 ILCS 710 — landlord must return within 30 days (45 if deductions)
- **Home repair contractors:** 815 ILCS 515 — must be licensed for jobs over $1,000
- **Consumer Fraud Act:** 815 ILCS 505 — deceptive business practices, may allow attorney fees
- **Statute of limitations:**
  - Written contracts: 10 years (735 ILCS 5/13-206)
  - Oral contracts: 5 years (735 ILCS 5/13-205)
  - Property damage: 5 years
