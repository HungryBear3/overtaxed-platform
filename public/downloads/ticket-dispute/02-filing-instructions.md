# Illinois Traffic Ticket Dispute — Step-by-Step Filing Instructions

---

## Before You Start

**Know your deadline.** Illinois municipalities have strict contest windows:
- **Chicago:** You must contest within **7 days** of the violation date (online or by mail), or request a hearing within **7 days** of a default judgment
- **Other Illinois municipalities:** Typically 10–30 days — check the back of your ticket
- **Missing the deadline = automatic guilty finding and late fees**

Set a calendar reminder right now.

---

## Step 1: Read Your Ticket Carefully

Find and note these key details from your citation:
1. **Ticket / Notice Number** — top of the ticket
2. **Violation date, time, and location**
3. **Violation type** — parking, red light camera, speed camera, moving violation
4. **Contest deadline** — usually printed on the back
5. **How to contest** — the ticket will list online, mail, or in-person options

---

## Step 2: Gather Your Evidence

Use the Evidence Guide (Document 3) to collect what you need. At minimum:
- A photo of the location if signage was the issue
- GPS/navigation records if you're disputing speed or location
- Any witness contact information

For **camera tickets** (red light / speed camera):
- You have the right to request the camera's calibration records
- Request them in writing before your hearing — it often leads to dismissal

---

## Step 3: Fill Out Your Dispute Letter

Use the template (Document 1) and:
- Check the applicable grounds for your contest
- Write a clear, factual 2–4 sentence statement
- List all attached evidence
- Sign and date it

Keep your tone professional. Hearing officers respond to facts, not frustration.

---

## Step 4: File Your Contest

### Chicago Parking / Camera Tickets
- **Online (fastest):** chicagoservices.chicago.gov → "Contest a Ticket"
- **By mail:** Chicago Dept. of Administrative Hearings, 400 W. Superior St., Chicago, IL 60654
- **In person:** 400 W. Superior St. or 321 N. Clark St.
- Attach your letter + any evidence (PDFs for online, copies for mail)

### Chicago Moving Violations (issued by CPD)
- These go to **Cook County Circuit Court**, Traffic Division
- Appear on your court date or request a continuance by mail
- Address: Richard J. Daley Center, 50 W. Washington, Chicago, IL 60602

### Collar County / Other Illinois Municipalities
- Check the back of your ticket for the specific filing address and method
- Most have online portals now — Google "[City name] contest traffic ticket"
- If no online option, mail your letter via **certified mail with return receipt** (keeps proof of filing date)

---

## Step 5: Attend Your Hearing (if scheduled)

Most camera ticket contests are decided on paper — no hearing required. But if one is scheduled:
- Arrive 15–20 minutes early
- Bring printed copies of all your evidence
- Speak clearly and stick to the facts — "The sign was not visible because X" not "This is unfair"
- Hearing officers have discretion to reduce fines even if they don't dismiss — always worth asking

---

## Common Winning Arguments

| Violation Type | Strong Defense |
|---|---|
| Red light camera | Camera calibration records, yellow light timing too short (must be ≥3 seconds in IL) |
| Speed camera | Calibration records, GPS data, school/park zone hours not in effect |
| Parking ticket | Sign missing/obscured, meter malfunction, medical emergency |
| Moving violation | Witness statements, dashcam footage, GPS data |

---

## If You Lose

- **Request a rehearing** within 35 days if new evidence emerges
- **Appeal to Circuit Court** — file within 35 days of the decision (small filing fee)
- **Negotiate a payment plan** if you can't pay — prevents license suspension

---

## Tips

✅ Always contest — the worst that happens is it stays the same

✅ Camera tickets: requesting calibration records signals you're serious and often prompts dismissal

✅ Never ignore a ticket — unpaid tickets in Illinois lead to license suspension and city stickers being denied in Chicago

✅ If your license is already suspended due to unpaid tickets, see a reinstatement attorney — it's fixable
