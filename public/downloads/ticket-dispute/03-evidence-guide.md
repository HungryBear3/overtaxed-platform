# Evidence Guide — Illinois Traffic Ticket Disputes

The right evidence turns a long-shot dispute into a likely win. This guide tells you exactly what to gather for each ticket type.

---

## For ALL Ticket Types

- **A copy of the ticket itself** — front and back
- **Photos of the exact location** taken as soon as possible after the violation
- **Your own notes** — write down what happened while it's fresh

---

## Parking Tickets

### Missing or Obscured Signage
- Photograph the sign (or lack of sign) from where your car was parked
- Photograph from the driver's perspective — if you couldn't see it from the car, document that angle
- Look for: missing signs, signs blocked by trees/trucks, signs facing the wrong direction, faded signs
- Note the nearest cross streets and exact address

### Meter Issues
- Photograph the meter display showing the malfunction
- Note the meter number (usually on a sticker on the meter post)
- Call 311 (Chicago) immediately to report a broken meter — this creates a record

### Street Cleaning / Permit Zone
- Photograph the posted schedule and your permit (if applicable)
- Check if the sign showed the correct days/times — Chicago signs sometimes list wrong info

---

## Red Light Camera Tickets

Red light cameras are a strong area to dispute. Camera errors and procedural violations are common.

### What to Request
Write to the issuing municipality requesting:
> "All calibration and maintenance records for camera unit [Camera ID on ticket] for the 90 days preceding [violation date], and documentation that the yellow light interval at this intersection meets the minimum 3-second requirement under Illinois law."

### Yellow Light Timing
- Illinois law requires yellow lights to be at minimum 3.0 seconds
- Cities have been caught setting them shorter to maximize ticket revenue
- If the yellow was short, the ticket must be dismissed
- Request the signal timing records from the city traffic engineering department

### Image Quality
- The ticket image must clearly show your license plate
- If the plate is blurry, partially obscured, or ambiguous — contest it
- State: "The image attached to this citation does not clearly and unambiguously identify my vehicle's license plate."

### Vehicle Not Yours
- If you sold the vehicle, provide bill of sale
- If someone else was driving, they can submit an Affidavit of Non-Liability naming the actual driver

## Speed Camera Tickets (Chicago School/Park Zones)

### Zone Hours
Chicago speed cameras only apply:
- **School zones:** 7 AM–7 PM on school days
- **Park zones:** 7 AM–7 PM daily (including weekends)

Verify the zone type and check if school was actually in session on that date. Winter break, summer, and holidays can be valid defenses.

### Camera Calibration
Same as red light — request calibration records. Speed cameras must be regularly certified. A gap in calibration records = strong dismissal argument.

### Speed Threshold
- Chicago school/park zone cameras: first offense fine for 6–10 mph over, higher for 11+ mph
- If you were just over the threshold, calibration error arguments carry extra weight

## Moving Violations (Officer-Issued)

### Dashcam Footage
- If you have a dashcam, preserve the footage immediately (many overwrite after 24–48 hours)
- Footage showing speed, position, and traffic conditions is powerful evidence

### GPS / Navigation Data
- Most smartphones log location and speed via Google Maps, Apple Maps, or Waze
- Google Timeline (maps.google.com/timeline) shows your route and speed history
- Screenshot and export the data for your violation date/time

### Witness Statements
- A passenger or nearby bystander can provide a written statement
- Include their name, contact info, and what they observed
- Signed and dated

### Officer's Notes
- You can request the officer's notes and any radar/LIDAR calibration records
- LIDAR/radar guns must be calibrated — a gap in records is a valid defense

---

## Organizing Your Evidence

Before filing, organize everything clearly:
1. Label each document (Exhibit A, Exhibit B, etc.)
2. Reference your exhibits in your dispute letter ("See Exhibit A — photograph of signage")
3. For online filing: scan or photograph as clear PDFs or JPGs
4. For mail: send copies only, never originals; send via certified mail

---

*The more organized and specific your submission, the more seriously it's taken. Hearing officers see hundreds of generic "I didn't do it" letters — yours should be factual, documented, and professional.*
